WCAG 2.2 Overview

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.2 is the latest iteration of the global standard for web accessibility. It includes all criteria from 2.0 and 2.1, adding 9 new success criteria designed to improve the web for users with cognitive disabilities, low vision, and those on mobile devices.

Key New Success Criteria

  • 2.4.11 Focus Not Obscured (AA): When a user focuses on an element, it must not be entirely hidden by other content.
  • 2.5.7 Dragging Movements (AA): Functionality that uses dragging must have a single-pointer alternative.
  • 2.5.8 Target Size (Minimum) (AA): The target size for pointer inputs should be at least 24x24 CSS pixels.

ADA Compliance Guide

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) prohibits discrimination based on disability. While the law was written in 1990—before the modern web—courts and the Department of Justice (DOJ) have affirmed that it applies to digital spaces.

Title III and Public Accommodations

Title III of the ADA covers "places of public accommodation." This originally meant physical locations like banks, hotels, and retail stores. However, in the digital age, a business's website and mobile app are considered extensions of their physical services (or public accommodations in their own right, depending on the court circuit).

DOJ Guidance (2022)

"The Department of Justice continues to take the position that web accessibility is a priority... Businesses open to the public must ensure that the programs, services, and activities they provide to the public are accessible to people with disabilities."

Common Legal Triggers

Most ADA lawsuits regarding digital accessibility cite specific barriers that prevent users from utilizing the service. Common triggers include:

  • Missing Alt Text: Screen reader users cannot understand the function of images or buttons.
  • Keyboard Traps: Users cannot navigate forms or purchase flows without a mouse.
  • Inaccessible PDFs: Essential documents (menus, contracts) are images rather than readable text.
  • Video Captions: Lack of closed captioning for deaf or hard-of-hearing users.

The "Safe Harbor" Standard

While the ADA does not codify specific technical standards, the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 Level AA is universally accepted by courts, the DOJ, and accessibility advocates as the benchmark for compliance.

Steps to Protect Your Organization

  1. Audit: Don't rely solely on automated widgets (overlays). Conduct a full manual audit of your core user flows.
  2. Remediate: Fix the code. Prioritize "blocker" issues that prevent access to primary services.
  3. Statement: Publish an Accessibility Statement. It shows good faith and provides a feedback mechanism for users to report issues before suing.
  4. Maintain: Accessibility is ongoing. Test every new deployment.

Mobile Accessibility

Mobile accessibility ensures apps and sites work on small screens, support touch gestures properly (with alternatives), and handle orientation changes (portrait/landscape) without breaking.

European Accessibility Act

The EAA (Directive 2019/882) applies to e-commerce, banking, and eBooks in the EU. Member states must enforce these rules by June 2025.

Keyboard Navigation

Everything must be operable by keyboard. Focus indicators must be visible. Tab order must be logical. No keyboard traps.

Screen Readers

Screen readers (NVDA, JAWS, VoiceOver) read semantic HTML. Using correct heading structures and landmark regions is crucial.
